## Challenge Description

Ludovico Casciarolo is the last known descendant of an old alchemical bloodline.
He believes the legendary glowing stone rose — once crafted by his ancestor — still exists, hidden somewhere in Bologna.

But it isn’t meant for everyone.

Only those who’ve walked the inner path — the alchemical journey through shadow, silence, and self — will be able to find it.

Through cryptic posts and symbolic clues, Ludovico is leaving fragments of the location behind.
Not to reveal the Rose, but to see who it calls.

## Overview

The challenge is based on a fictional narrative created around Ludovico Casciaroli, a descendant of the 17th-century alchemist Vincenzo Casciarolo, known for discovering the luminescent “Bologna Stone.”

According to Ludovico’s writings, a symbolic object — the Stone Rose — is hidden somewhere in the city of Bologna. But locating it requires more than solving riddles or following coordinates. It demands a personal journey of interpretation, patience, and self-reflection.

Participants were invited to follow a series of cryptic posts written by Ludovico, each tied to one of Bologna’s “Seven Secrets.” These posts blended poetic imagery with historical references and symbolic language. Each contained a hidden clue pointing — directly or indirectly — to a single location: the Archiginnasio of Bologna, one of the oldest seats of learning in Europe, and the final resting place of the Rose.

### 🔍 Breakdown of the Clues (ignore posts not related to seven secrets of Bologna)

---

### 1. **Via Piella’s Little Window**

This post begins with a reflection on the hidden canal visible through the Finestrella in Via Piella — a symbol of what lies beneath the surface, both literally and metaphorically. Ludovico uses the canal as a metaphor for hidden knowledge, the kind that isn’t seen in the open streets but behind closed walls.

The real clue, however, is in the second half of the post:

- “A palace of coats and carved ceilings” refers explicitly to the Archiginnasio, where hundreds of student family crests (stemmi) cover the walls, and ornate ceilings — especially in the Teatro Anatomico and the Sala dello Stabat Mater — dominate the interior.
- “Where books listen more than they speak” alludes to the library and to the atmosphere of contemplative study typical of the Archiginnasio: a place of silence, reflection, and preserved knowledge.
- “The crest with no name” is likely a reference to one of the many incomplete or eroded stemmi within the Archiginnasio. These missing names suggest forgotten knowledge or lost identity — again, tying into the theme of hidden or partial understanding.
- “Look up. You’ll see what I saw. The stars. Inside the wood.” points to the coffered ceiling of the Teatro Anatomico, where zodiacal and astral symbols are carved into wood — a reference to ancient medical practices, astrology, and the unity of microcosm and macrocosm in alchemical thought.

### 2. **The Fountain of Neptune**

> "That finger — that minute hand — is aimed directly toward Via d’Azeglio, a street walked by students and strangers alike, but only a few of them ever bother to look at where the hand is pointing.
> If the fountain is a clock, then the time is eleven.
> Eleven o’clock."

Imagine that the Fontana del Nettuno is the center of a clock.
The finger of Neptune’s statue becomes the minute hand, and it points toward Via d’Azeglio.

If we imagine an analog clock face superimposed over the city with its center on the fountain, the Archiginnasio would lie roughly in the direction of 11 o’clock.

So:

- Minute hand = finger = Via d’Azeglio
- Hour hand = Archiginnasio direction = 11

Ludovico says: “If the fountain is a clock, then the time is eleven.”
That time is not random — it's symbolic: the direction of 11 o’clock on this imaginary city-clock points toward the Archiginnasio.

### 3. **The Three Arrows (Corte Isolani)**

> _“It didn’t aim for flesh. Didn’t care for vengeance. It followed a different path. Southwest. Through the cloisters, past the empty chapels, cutting across time like a needle.”_ > _“If you trace the angle, if you walk it with patience, you’ll find a place with no arrows left — just wounds shaped like questions.”_

Ludovico refers to the **legend of the three arrows** stuck in the wooden beams of Corte Isolani. Ludovico speaks of a **“fourth arrow”**, one that was never seen — because, unlike the others, it **followed a different path**.

The key phrase here is:

> _“It followed a different path. Southwest.”_

If you open a map and locate **Corte Isolani**, and then look toward the **southwest**, you’ll find that a straight line in that direction leads to the **Archiginnasio**.

Ludovico is inviting the reader to imagine this fourth arrow as **a trajectory through the city**. He reinforces this by saying:

> _“Through the cloisters, past the empty chapels...”_ — possibly referring to passing **Santo Stefano** or other old religious buildings on the way.
> _“Cutting across time like a needle”_ — indicating a symbolic line, or timeline, through Bologna’s history.

### 4. **Voltone del Podestà (Whispering Gallery)**

The audio playing on the site is Stabat Mater by Rossini — a reference to the Sala dello Stabat Mater, a historic hall inside the Archiginnasio.

### 5. **Canton dei Fiori (Panis Vita...)**

The post contrasts the street’s motto with the city’s real foundation: the ancient Studium of Bologna.
Ludovico is referring to the Archiginnasio, which was once the official seat of the Studium

### 6. **Palazzo Poggi**

> _“And yet, if you listen closely, the walls don’t sound like firsts — they echo like seconds.
> As if something older once held the title. Something that taught before this place was even planned.
> […] Fifteen minutes, heading southwest. Past the towers. Past the squares. Just instinct and the weight of forgotten footsteps.
> And there it was.
> A building with walls covered in hundreds of family crests. A theatre built entirely in wood, where silence still breathes. A hall where lectures were sung like rituals.
> Something is close. And it listens from under a ceiling full of stars.”_

In this post, Ludovico is standing at **Palazzo Poggi**, which is the current seat of the University of Bologna. However, he makes it clear that this place is **not the original Studium** — it is a **"second"**, not a "first".

He recalls that the original Studium — the true birthplace of learning in Bologna — existed **before Palazzo Poggi**. He then describes an **imaginary walk**:

- **“Fifteen minutes, heading southwest”** is an exact clue — that’s the real walking distance between Palazzo Poggi and the **Archiginnasio**.

He then describes the destination in detail:

- **“A building with walls covered in hundreds of family crests”** → clearly refers to the **Archiginnasio**, where the walls are decorated with over 6,000 student stemmi.
- **“A theatre built entirely in wood”** → points to the **Teatro Anatomico**, the wooden lecture hall.
- **“A hall where lectures were sung like rituals”** → refers to the **Sala dello Stabat Mater**, used for solemn academic ceremonies.
- **“A ceiling full of stars”** → alludes to the **astrological ceiling** above the Anatomical Theatre.

### 7. **Torre degli Asinelli (Broken Vase)**

The note found in the broken vase references a medical student from 1650, a time when the Archiginnasio was still the official seat of the University of Bologna, including its School of Medicine.
The clue — “where the sky is clearest… back to the room beneath the ceiling full of constellations” — points directly to the Teatro Anatomico, where medicine was taught under a ceiling decorated with stars

---

### Final Destination: Archiginnasio

Each of these clues, taken together, led to a **single location**: the **Archiginnasio of Bologna** (44.492, 11.343)
